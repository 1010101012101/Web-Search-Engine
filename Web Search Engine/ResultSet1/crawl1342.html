Unemployment Rate Drops to 7.0 Percent



The economy added 203,000 jobs in November as the unemployment rate dropped 0.3 percentage points. While this report is largely positive, it is important to note that at the current rate of job growth, it would take six years for the economy to make up for the jobs lost to to the downturn. The Honduran Elections As They Happened



Thousands of people followed along as CEPR live-blogged Honduras' elections on Sunday, November 24. These were Honduras' first elections since the controversial 2009 vote that was held less than five months after a coup d'etat deposed the democratically-elected president Manuel Zelaya. See our Honduras issue page for background information, as well as Senior Associate for International Policy Alexander Main's op-ed in the Los Angeles Times.

Policy Alternatives for a Return to Full Employment in Spain



A new CEPR paper describes how, contrary to claims by Spanish Prime Minister Mariano Rajoy that “there is no other alternative” to austerity, there are feasible policy changes that would allow Spain to increase growth and restore full employment by 2018 or even sooner, without increasing debt levels.

New Book by Dean Baker and Jared Bernstein



In Getting Back to Full Employment: A Better Bargain for Working People, Baker and Bernstein make the case that low levels of unemployment benefit the economy and make it possible for millions more workers to get a fair share of the nation's economic growth.

Affordable Care Act Begins Covering Uninsured

CEPR Co-Director Dean Baker has a column on Truthout on the state-level exchanges kicking in, which allow the uninsured to be covered under the Affordable Care Act. In August, Dean and Helene Jorgensen found no evidence supporting the claim that the shortening of workweeks in response to the mandate has been a widespread phenomenon.

New Paper Finds that Honduras’ Economic and Social Gains Under Zelaya Were Largely Reversed After the Coup

A new CEPR paper finds that economic inequality in Honduras has increased dramatically since the 2009 coup, while poverty has worsened, unemployment has increased and underemployment has risen sharply, with many more workers receiving less than the minimum wage. The Los Angeles Times wrote an article on the paper's findings, here.
